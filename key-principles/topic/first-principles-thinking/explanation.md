# First Principles Thinking

## What first principles thinking means

First principles thinking is a way of understanding a problem by breaking it down into its most basic truths and then building back up from those truths. A **principle** is a foundational statement that remains true even when opinions, habits, traditions, and assumptions are stripped away. A **first principle** is a starting truth that does not depend on some other idea inside the current discussion.

In plain language, first principles thinking means:

1. Identify what you are trying to understand or solve.
2. Separate facts from assumptions.
3. Reduce the problem to its essential parts.
4. Rebuild a solution from those essential parts.

This method is useful because people often reason by analogy. Reasoning by analogy means copying what already exists or repeating what usually works. That can be efficient, but it can also preserve bad assumptions. First principles thinking is slower at the beginning, but it can reveal simpler, clearer, and more original answers.

## Why it matters

Many problems stay confusing because they are wrapped in inherited language, accepted practices, and vague goals. First principles thinking helps remove that fog.

It is valuable because it helps you:

- understand a problem more deeply instead of memorizing surface patterns
- challenge assumptions that may no longer be true
- design solutions that fit the real problem
- avoid copying weak decisions from previous work
- explain your reasoning clearly to other people

When used well, first principles thinking improves both learning and decision-making. It is not only for philosophy, science, or engineering. It is useful anywhere a person needs to move from confusion to clarity.

## The difference between facts, assumptions, and conventions

To use first principles thinking well, it helps to distinguish three kinds of statements.

### Facts

A **fact** is something treated as true within the current problem because it can be directly observed, logically established, or explicitly defined.

Examples:

- A document needs words in order to communicate ideas.
- A user can only follow instructions they can understand.
- A process with too many steps usually takes more time than a process with fewer steps.

### Assumptions

An **assumption** is something accepted without being fully tested in the current situation.

Examples:

- People will already know the background terms.
- The current format is the best format.
- A longer document is automatically more complete.

Assumptions are not always wrong, but they should not be treated as foundational truths until examined.

### Conventions

A **convention** is a common way of doing something. Conventions can be useful because they create consistency, but they are not the same as first principles.

Examples:

- naming files a certain way
- organizing documents into standard sections
- using a familiar workflow because the team already knows it

A convention can be kept, changed, or discarded depending on whether it serves the underlying goal.

## The core method

A practical first principles process can be expressed in five stages.

### 1. State the actual problem

Write the problem in simple language. Avoid vague wording.

Weak problem statement:

> We need better documentation.

Stronger problem statement:

> We need documentation that allows a new reader to understand a topic without needing outside context.

A precise statement matters because a vague problem produces vague reasoning.

### 2. List what is known

Write down what is definitely true for the problem. These become the base materials for reasoning.

For example, if you are designing a topic document, you may know:

- the reader must be able to understand it without external references
- the document should cover one topic clearly
- the content should be organized in a predictable location
- different content types serve different reader needs

These are closer to first principles than statements like "copy what most docs do."

### 3. Expose hidden assumptions

Ask questions such as:

- What am I taking for granted?
- What is only true because it is familiar?
- What would break if this assumption were false?
- Am I confusing a habit with a necessity?

This step is important because many weak solutions come from untested assumptions rather than missing effort.

### 4. Break the problem into parts

Decompose the problem into smaller pieces that can be reasoned about separately.

For example, a documentation problem may contain these subproblems:

- what the reader needs to know
- what order the ideas should appear in
- what definitions must be included
- what examples make the idea concrete
- what structure best fits the content type

Once the parts are visible, each part becomes easier to test and improve.

### 5. Rebuild from the ground up

Now form a solution using the basic truths you identified rather than the defaults you inherited.

Instead of saying, "This is how we usually write it," say:

- The reader needs definitions before advanced ideas.
- The reader needs examples to connect abstract concepts to reality.
- The reader should not need outside knowledge.
- Therefore, the document must define terms, explain the logic, and include examples in the same file.

This is the rebuilding step. It converts insight into design.

## A simple mental model

You can think of first principles thinking as three repeated questions:

1. **What is the goal?**
2. **What must be true?**
3. **What follows from those truths?**

If you keep cycling through those questions, you gradually replace borrowed thinking with direct reasoning.

## Worked example: understanding a bicycle

A bicycle is a useful example because it is familiar.

If someone reasons by analogy, they might say:

- A bicycle is built like other bicycles.
- It should look the way bicycles usually look.
- We should keep the same shape because that is normal.

If someone reasons from first principles, they ask:

- What is the goal of the object?
- What must it do for that goal to be achieved?

The goal might be:

> move a person efficiently using human power while allowing balance and steering.

From there, essential needs become clearer:

- the rider must transfer force into motion
- the system must roll with low friction
- the rider must control direction
- the rider must remain balanced or be stabilized
- the structure must support the rider's weight

Once those truths are visible, the familiar bicycle design stops being the starting point. It becomes one possible answer among many. That shift is the essence of first principles thinking.

## Worked example: writing self-contained documentation

Consider the problem:

> Create content that a reader can understand without outside knowledge.

A shallow approach may copy the structure of existing documents and assume the reader already knows the basic ideas.

A first principles approach asks:

- What is the actual goal?
- What must be true for that goal to be met?

Possible first principles:

- A reader cannot understand undefined terms.
- A reader cannot follow reasoning if key steps are missing.
- A reader benefits from examples when ideas are abstract.
- A document is easier to use when it has one clear purpose.

From those principles, design decisions follow:

- define every important term inside the document
- explain the topic from simple to complex
- include examples in the same file
- avoid requiring external links just to understand the basics
- keep the document focused on one topic and one content type

This is why self-contained content is not just a style preference. It follows logically from the goal of reader understanding.

## How to tell when you are not using first principles

You are probably not thinking from first principles if your reasoning sounds like this:

- "That is just how it is done."
- "Everyone already knows that part."
- "We should keep it because that is the standard way."
- "It worked before, so it must be right now."
- "We do not need to define it because the audience will figure it out."

These statements may sometimes lead to acceptable results, but they do not prove that the result is well-founded.

## Benefits and trade-offs

First principles thinking is powerful, but it is not free.

### Benefits

- produces deeper understanding
- reveals hidden assumptions
- supports original problem solving
- improves explanations and decision quality
- helps teams discuss reasoning rather than defend habits

### Trade-offs

- takes more time at the start
- can feel uncomfortable because it questions familiar practices
- may generate more options than a team can immediately evaluate
- can become unproductive if used on trivial decisions that do not need deep analysis

Good judgment means using first principles when the problem is important, unclear, constrained, or full of assumptions.

## Common mistakes

### Mistake 1: treating opinions as fundamentals

Not every strongly stated belief is a first principle. A real first principle should survive scrutiny.

### Mistake 2: breaking things down without rebuilding

Analysis alone is not enough. The goal is not merely to deconstruct a problem. The goal is to create a better solution from the deeper understanding.

### Mistake 3: ignoring real constraints

First principles thinking does not mean pretending limits do not exist. Time, cost, skill, and context are still real. The method helps you reason clearly within constraints, not escape them.

### Mistake 4: using it everywhere

Some problems do not need deep reconstruction. If a standard approach already fits the goal and has no harmful assumptions, using it may be sensible.

## A repeatable checklist

Use this checklist when applying first principles thinking:

1. What is the real goal?
2. What does success mean in plain language?
3. What facts are clearly true?
4. What assumptions am I making?
5. Which assumptions can be removed or tested?
6. What are the smallest important parts of the problem?
7. What solution follows if I build only from the essential truths?
8. Is the result understandable, defensible, and practical?

## When this topic should be used

This topic is most useful when:

- a problem feels stuck because people keep repeating habits
- a team needs to simplify something complicated
- a writer needs to explain an idea from the ground up
- a decision must be justified clearly rather than defended by precedent

## Final summary

First principles thinking is the discipline of starting from the most basic truths available, separating those truths from assumptions, and building a solution from the ground up. Its purpose is not to be clever or abstract. Its purpose is to improve understanding and produce stronger reasoning.

A person using first principles thinking does not ask only, "What do people usually do?" They also ask, "What must be true, and what follows from that?" That question is often the difference between repeating a pattern and genuinely understanding a problem.
